# HADRIAN'S WALL: Summary Handout
## Construction, Purpose, and Legacy (122-128 AD)

---

## Quick Facts
- **Length:** 73 miles (117 km) coast-to-coast across northern Britain
- **Construction Period:** 122-128 AD (6-8 years)
- **Builders:** 15,000 soldiers from three Roman legions
  - Legio II Augusta
  - Legio VI Victrix  
  - Legio XX Valeria Victrix
- **Height:** 15-20 feet (4.5-6 m)
- **Width:** Originally 10 feet, reduced to 6-8 feet mid-construction
- **Structures:** 80 milecastles, 160 turrets, 16 major forts

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ How reliable are these sources?

**‚ö†Ô∏è Major Problems:**

**BIAS:**
- All sources are Roman‚Äîno British perspective exists in writing
- Word choice reveals prejudice: "barbaros" (barbarians) = propaganda term
- Portrays Romans as civilized saviors, Britons as savages needing control

**EXAGGERATION:**
- Historia Augusta written 180+ years after events, notoriously unreliable
- Mixes fact and fiction, creates heroic narratives
- Emphasizes military glory, ignores costs and failures

**INCOMPLETENESS:**
- Sources don't mention construction plan changes (archaeology reveals this)
- Silent on forced labor and displacement of British locals
- Omit economic function (taxation/customs barrier)
- Ignore British resistance and resentment

**‚öñÔ∏è More Reliable Sources:**
- **Centurial stones:** Primary evidence from construction period
- **Vindolanda tablets:** Unfiltered daily life, not propaganda
- **Tombstones:** Personal records, less biased

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ What does archaeology confirm or contradict?

| CONFIRMED ‚úì | CONTRADICTED OR COMPLICATED ‚úó |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Wall really is 73 miles long, coast-to-coast | "Absolute separation" is false‚Äîgates every mile for controlled passage |

**Archaeological Discoveries:**
- **Broad Wall vs. Narrow Wall:** Physical evidence shows original plan called for 10-foot-wide wall, reduced to 6-8 feet to speed construction. Some sections already built remained wide, creating visible difference today.
- **Milecastles with gates on both sides:** Soldiers could pass through wall easily‚Äîit controlled movement, not prevented it.
- **Mixed Roman-British artifacts:** Pottery, jewelry, and tools show cultural blending, trade, intermarriage.
- **Vindolanda wooden tablets:** Waterlogged soil preserved thin wood slices with ink writing‚Äîunprecedented window into daily life.

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ How did occupation affect Romans and Britons?

#### Impact on ROMANS üèõÔ∏è

**Military:**
- 10,000+ soldiers permanently deployed on harsh, remote frontier
- Troops from Mediterranean suffered in cold British climate
- 25-year service terms meant soldiers never returned home

**Economic:**
- Massive resource drain‚Äîconstruction and maintenance costs
- Britain never profitable enough to justify expense
- Supply lines stretched across empire

**Social:**
- Soldiers married local women, had children (Romano-British families)
- Cultural isolation‚Äîhomesickness evident in Vindolanda tablets
- Some soldiers (like Annius Davicus) were actually recruited Britons

**Political:**
- Hadrian's defensive policy controversial in Rome
- Seen as abandoning conquest and glory-seeking
- Created tensions with senatorial class

---

#### Impact on BRITONS üõ°Ô∏è

**Negative:**
- **Land seizure:** Thousands of acres confiscated for wall and military zone
- **Displacement:** Villages and farms destroyed, families separated
- **Forced labor:** Likely conscripted locals for construction (sources silent on this)
- **Tribal division:** Wall split northern tribes from southern, disrupting kinship and trade
- **Economic exploitation:** Wall functioned as customs barrier‚Äîtaxation on movement
- **Cultural suppression:** Roman religion, language, law imposed; traditional ways marginalized
- **Loss of sovereignty:** Conquered people with no voice in their own governance

**Positive (for some):**
- **Military service:** Britons could join auxiliary units, earn citizenship after 25 years
- **Trade opportunities:** Forts became market towns, creating new economies
- **Technology transfer:** Roman engineering, agriculture, medicine, writing introduced
- **Social mobility:** Some Britons prospered under Roman rule

**‚öñÔ∏è Complex Reality:** Not simply oppressor vs. oppressed. Colonial relationship with unequal power but also cultural exchange, economic integration, and hybrid Romano-British identity.

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ What long-term legacy did this leave?

#### Geographic Divide
- Wall created lasting north-south boundary in Britain
- Scottish-English distinctions partly follow this ancient Roman frontier
- Even today, cultural and linguistic differences align with wall's location

#### Infrastructure
- Roman roads connecting forts became medieval highways
- Modern roads (A69, B6318) follow Roman routes
- Towns grew where forts stood: Carlisle, Newcastle, Corbridge

#### Language
- Latin words entered English, especially place names
- **"-chester" and "-caster"** from Latin *castra* (fort): Newcastle, Manchester, Lancaster, Winchester, Doncaster, Leicester, Colchester, etc.
- Hundreds of place names preserve Roman origins

#### Cultural Memory
- Wall became symbol of Britain's Roman past
- Victorian Britain saw themselves as Rome's successors (justifying their own empire)
- Inspired art, literature, tourism for 2000 years
- **George R.R. Martin:** Cited Hadrian's Wall as inspiration for "The Wall" in *Game of Thrones*

#### Modern Tourism & Identity
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site** (1987)
- Northern England's top tourist attraction (¬£20+ million annually)
- **Hadrian's Wall Path:** 84-mile walking trail (opened 2003)
- Source of regional pride and identity
- Best-preserved Roman frontier in the world

#### Archaeological Treasure
- Hundreds of inscriptions, artifacts, structures
- Vindolanda tablets = most important Roman documents found in Britain
- Ongoing excavations continue to reveal new information
- Teaches modern scholars about Roman military, engineering, daily life

## Critical Analysis: The "Barbaros" Problem

### The Quote:
> *"Britanniam muro separavit, qui barbaros Romanosque divideret."*

### Why This Matters:

**1. Loaded Language**
- *"Barbaros"* = "barbarians" (anyone non-Greek/Roman who spoke incomprehensible languages‚Äî"bar-bar" sounds)
- Creates binary: civilized Romans vs. savage Britons
- Justifies conquest and occupation

**2. False Narrative**
- Britons had complex societies, trade networks, art, religion, governance
- Many tribes were Romanized already, traded with empire
- Some served in Roman army‚Äînot exactly "barbarians"

**3. Propaganda Function**
- Makes wall seem defensive ("protecting" civilization)
- Reality: offensive tool of imperial control
- Divides people along ethnic lines to prevent unified resistance

**4. Archaeological Contradiction**
- Gates every mile = not absolute separation
- Mixed settlements = Romans and Britons living together
- Intermarriage = cultural blending, not division

**üí° Lesson:** Primary sources are evidence of *how Romans thought*, not objective truth. Always question word choices, consider missing perspectives, compare with physical evidence.

## Construction Timeline: 4 Major Phases

### Phase 1: Planning (122 AD)
- Emperor Hadrian visits Britain, orders wall
- Surveyors map route from coast to coast
- Choose strategic high ground

### Phase 2: Eastern Stone Wall (122-124 AD)
- Three legions begin simultaneously
- Eastern section (Newcastle to River Irthing) built in stone
- Original width: 10 Roman feet (3 m)
- Milecastles and turrets added

### Phase 3: Western Turf Wall & Plan Change (124-126 AD)
- Western section initially built in turf (faster)
- **MAJOR REVISION:** Width reduced to 6-8 feet to speed work
- Already-built sections kept original width ‚Üí "Broad Wall" vs. "Narrow Wall"

### Phase 4: Forts & Vallum (126-128 AD)
- 16 large forts added *onto* the wall (original plan: behind wall)
- Vallum (large ditch system) dug south of wall
- Turf sections gradually replaced with stone
- Wall whitewashed (visible for miles‚Äîshow of power)

**Continued modifications for 300+ years of Roman occupation!**
