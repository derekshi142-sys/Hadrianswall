## SLIDE 2: Latin Source 1 - "Barbaros Romanosque" (2 minutes)

### Read the Latin Aloud:
*"Britanniam muro separavit, qui barbaros Romanosque divideret."*

### Then Give Translation:
"He separated Britain with a wall, to divide the barbarians from the Romans."

### What to Emphasize:
**The word "BARBAROS" is crucial here.**

### Points to Make:
1. This is from the Historia Augusta‚Äîwritten about 180 years AFTER Hadrian
2. "Barbaros" is loaded propaganda language‚Äînot objective
3. Creates false binary: civilized vs. savage
4. Justifies Roman occupation by painting Britons as dangerous outsiders

### Analysis to Share:
*"But here's the problem: the Britons weren't mindless barbarians. They had complex societies, trade networks, and culture. Many already traded with Rome and some served in Roman auxiliary units. This word choice tells us more about Roman bias than historical reality."*

### Archaeological Challenge:
*"And archaeology contradicts this 'separation' narrative‚Äîthe wall had gates every single mile. It controlled movement for taxation and customs, not absolute division."*

### Transition:
"This biased language appears throughout Roman sources. Let's see how Hadrian's overall approach to the empire..."

## SLIDE 3: Latin Source 2 - Hadrian's Policy (1 minute)

### Read the Latin:
*"Circumeunti provincias multa correxit, multa mutavit."*

### Translation:
"While touring the provinces, he corrected many things and changed many things."

### Key Point:
**Hadrian was a reformer, not a conqueror.**

### What to Say:
*"The verbs 'correxit' (corrected) and 'mutavit' (changed) show Hadrian breaking from his predecessor Trajan. Trajan was all about expansion‚Äîconquering new territory. Hadrian thought that was unsustainable. He abandoned conquests in the East and built defensive walls instead. The wall represents this new policy: fix the border, consolidate what you have, stop overextending."*

### Note:
*"This was controversial in Rome! The Senate and military elite thought giving up territory was weak. But Hadrian prioritized stability over glory."*

## SLIDE 4: Latin Source 3 - Centurial Stones (1.5 minutes)

### Show the Latin:
*"LEG II AVG FEC"*

### Translation:
"The Second Augustan Legion made this."

### What to Say:
*"Now this is a primary source‚Äîactual inscriptions carved into wall stones by the soldiers who built them. These 'centurial stones' marked sections built by specific centuries‚Äîunits of about 80 men within a legion."*

### Key Facts:
- Three legions: II Augusta, VI Victrix, XX Valeria Victrix
- "FEC" = fecit = "made this"
- Shows pride in workmanship
- Competition between units

### Important Point:
*"Roman soldiers weren't just fighters‚Äîthey were engineers, surveyors, builders. This versatility is why Rome could build massive projects in remote locations. The wall is as much an engineering achievement as a military one."*

## SLIDE 5: Latin Source 4 - Tombstone (1.5 minutes)

### Read the Latin:
*"D M ANNIVS DAVICVS MILITAVIT ANNOS XXV VIXIT ANNOS XLV"*

### Translation:
"To the spirits of the departed. Annius Davicus, served 25 years, lived 45 years."

### Humanize It:
*"This is where the history becomes personal. Annius Davicus spent his entire adult life‚Äîfrom age 20 to 45‚Äîserving on this cold, remote frontier. And notice his name: 'Davicus' is Celtic, not Roman. He was a local Briton recruited into Roman service."*

### What This Shows:
1. **Human cost:** Real people lived and died here
2. **Mixed identity:** Conquered people serving their conquerors
3. **Harsh conditions:** Died at 45‚Äînot old, but frontier life was brutal
4. **Religious continuity:** "D M" (Dis Manibus) shows Roman religious practices even in remote Britain

### Emotion:
*"Tombstones like this remind us that behind every statistic‚Äî'15,000 soldiers'‚Äîwere real individuals with families, hopes, and hardships."*

## SLIDE 6: Latin Source 5 - Vindolanda Tablet (1.5 minutes)

### Read the Latin:
*"Cetera mitto tibi... subligaria duo... tunicam... cervicellas duas"*

### Translation:
"I send you other items... two pairs of underpants... a tunic... two pairs of socks"

### Audience Hook:
*"Yes, you heard that right‚Äîa 2000-year-old request for underwear and socks! This is from the famous Vindolanda tablets, thin wooden slices preserved in waterlogged soil."*

### What This Reveals:
1. **Conditions were brutal:** Northern Britain was freezing for Mediterranean troops
2. **Supply chains:** Families sent care packages from home
3. **Daily reality:** Not epic battles, but mundane survival
4. **Humanity:** These soldiers worried about the same things we do‚Äîstaying warm, dry, comfortable

### Contrast:
*"This contrasts sharply with propaganda sources like the Historia Augusta, which present heroic narratives. The tablets show real life‚Äîbirthday party invitations, complaints about roads, requests for beer. It's incredibly humanizing."*

## SLIDE 7: Latin Source 6 - Hadrian's Management (1.5 minutes)

### Read the Latin:
*"Exercitum per centuriones ac tribunos militares quam per legatos habebat disciplinae firmioris."*

### Translation:
"He kept the army through centurions and military tribunes rather than through legates, as it was more firmly disciplined."

### Key Phrase:
**"disciplinae firmioris" = "of firmer discipline"**

### Analysis:
*"Hadrian micromanaged the wall project. He used centurions‚Äîexperienced NCOs‚Äîand tribunes instead of high-ranking legates. Why? Centurions were practical builders, not aristocrats. This ensured consistent quality across 73 miles."*

### Evidence:
*"And it worked! The wall shows remarkable engineering consistency‚Äîsame dimensions, regular spacing of structures. That's micromanagement paying off."*

### Political Angle:
*"But this also shows Hadrian didn't trust the senatorial class to do the job right. That created political enemies back in Rome."*

## SLIDE 8: Latin Source 7 - Land Confiscation (2 minutes)

### Read the Latin:
*"Agrum publicum per omnes provincias aut occupatum a privatis aut vacua possessione interceptum recepit."*

### Translation:
"Throughout all the provinces, he reclaimed public land that had been seized by private individuals or taken through vacant possession."

### Critical Analysis:
**Focus on: "occupatum a privatis" = "seized by private individuals"**

### Two Perspectives:
*"From Rome's viewpoint, this is reclaiming 'public' land. But from the Britons' perspective? This is theft of ancestral territory."*

### What Really Happened:
1. Wall needed ~73-mile footprint PLUS military zone on both sides
2. British farms, villages, sacred sites destroyed
3. Families displaced
4. No compensation, no choice

### Irony:
*"The passive verb 'occupatum'‚Äîseized/occupied‚Äîis ironic. The Romans accuse private individuals of 'seizing' land, but Rome is the ultimate occupier, taking an entire country by force."*

### Impact:
*"This land seizure created lasting resentment. The wall wasn't just a military structure‚Äîit was a symbol of dispossession."*

## SLIDE 9: Latin Source 8 - British Resistance (2 minutes)

### Read the Latin:
*"Britanni tolerare ultra nequirent Romanorum iniurias."*

### Translation:
"The Britons could no longer endure the injustices of the Romans."

### Context Note:
*"Now, this quote is actually about Boudicca's Revolt in 60-61 AD, about 60 years before the wall. But it illustrates ongoing tensions throughout Roman occupation."*

### Key Phrase:
**"tolerare ultra nequirent" = "could no longer endure"**

### What to Say:
*"This is a breaking point. The phrase captures accumulating grievances: land seizure, taxation, forced labor, cultural suppression. 'Iniurias'‚Äîinjustices or outrages‚Äîis a strong word. And the wall itself was one of those injustices."*

### Archaeological Evidence:
- Northern villages abandoned during wall construction
- Tribal territories split
- Trade routes disrupted
- Families divided

### Missing Voices:
*"And here's the crucial point: this is still a Roman source‚ÄîCassius Dio writing about what Britons felt. We have NO written sources from Britons themselves. Their voices are completely absent. Only archaeology gives us hints of their experience."*

## SLIDE 10: Construction Timeline (2 minutes)

### Overview:
*"Now that we've examined the sources, let's look at how the wall was actually built. It wasn't a single, smooth process‚Äîplans changed dramatically midway through."*

### Walk Through the Timeline:
**122 AD:** Planning phase‚Äîsurveyors map route, Hadrian approves design

**122-124 AD:** Eastern section built in stone, original width 10 feet

**124-126 AD:** Big change! Width reduced to 6-8 feet to speed up construction. Western section initially built in turf (faster).

**126-128 AD:** Forts added directly ONTO the wall (original plan had them behind). Vallum dug.

**128-138 AD:** Turf sections replaced with stone. Wall whitewashed‚Äîvisible for miles as a show of power.

### Key Point:
*"The wall was never really 'finished'‚Äîmodifications continued for 300 years of Roman occupation!"*

## Anticipated Questions & Answers

### Q: "Why did Hadrian build the wall instead of just conquering Scotland?"
**A:** *"Scotland (Caledonia) was mountainous, sparsely populated, and economically unproductive. Hadrian was pragmatic‚Äîconquering it would cost more than it was worth. His successor Antoninus Pius tried building another wall further north (Antonine Wall), but it was abandoned after 20 years. Hadrian was right‚ÄîScotland wasn't worth the cost."*

### Q: "Did the wall actually work? Did it stop invasions?"
**A:** *"Depends what you mean by 'work.' It didn't stop all raids‚ÄîCaledonians could bypass it or attack the coast. But it controlled movement, allowed taxation of trade, and demonstrated Roman power. It was more about administration and display than pure military defense. In that sense, yes, it worked for 300 years."*

### Q: "How much of the wall still exists?"
**A:** *"About 10% of the original structure is still standing. Much was robbed for building material over centuries‚Äîchurches, castles, and farms used the stones. But foundations and lower courses exist along most of the route, and some sections like Steel Rigg stand up to 10 feet high. UNESCO protection since 1987 has helped preserve what remains."*

### Q: "Were there any famous battles at the wall?"
**A:** *"Not really. The wall wasn't designed for large-scale battles‚Äîit was for patrol and control. Most military action was small raids or skirmishes. The wall's success was preventing large-scale conflict through deterrence and early warning systems (the turrets). Boring but effective!"*

### Q: "What happened to the wall after Romans left?"
**A:** *"After Roman withdrawal in 410 AD, locals continued using the forts as settlements. The wall gradually fell into disrepair, and stones were extensively robbed. By medieval times, people didn't even know who built it‚Äîthey called it 'Picts' Wall' or 'Devil's Wall.' It wasn't until 18th-century antiquarians identified it as Roman and began studying it scientifically."*

### Q: "Did the Romans build other walls?"
**A:** *"Yes! The Antonine Wall in Scotland (further north, abandoned after 20 years), walls along the German frontier (the Limes), and walls in North Africa. But Hadrian's Wall is the best-preserved and most impressive. It's part of the 'Frontiers of the Roman Empire' UNESCO World Heritage Site."*

### Q: "Were all the soldiers on the wall Roman?"
**A:** *"No! Most were auxiliaries‚Äînon-Roman troops from across the empire. We have evidence of Spanish cavalry, Belgian infantry, Syrian archers, and many local Britons. Only the officers and legionaries were full Roman citizens. This multi-ethnic garrison shows how diverse Rome's military was."*
